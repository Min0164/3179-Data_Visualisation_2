Suicide is defined as a death caused by intentional self-harm.

The World Health Organization (WHO) and the Global Burden of Diseases study estimate that almost 800,000 people die from suicide every year, that is one person every 40 seconds, which shows that suicide has become a serious public health issue and preventive measures need to be taken.

The main focus of this visualization is the Suicide Rate in Europe. This visualisation shows how the suicide rate in Europe changed over the year from 1985 to 2015, factors related to the suicide rate, and the suicide rate in different groups of people by gender and age.

Overall Trend of Suicide Rate in Europe from 1985 to 2015

From year 1990 onwards, we can see that Eastern Europe(EE) which is less developed than other region of Europe has relatively high suicide rate especially Lithuania This shows that the development of a country may potentially effect the suicide rate.

The overall trend of the suicide rate for year between 1895 and 1899 was not shown due to the lack of data



Click on the country in the map to observe the trend of the clicked country over the year in the line chart.

From the line, it is apparent that average suicide rate per 100,000 population in Europe did not show significant changes, ranging between 13.56 and 19.37.

With each point representing a different nation, we can see that the majority of the nation has a lower suicide rate that is lower than average

The suicide rate per 100,000 people for each year show high variation with some outliers. For instance, Lithuania in 2000.

Top 10 countries with the highest suicide rate over the years



From the bump chart, we can see that Lithuania had the highest suicide rate in Europe for 21 consecutive years, starting from 1995 to 2015 since its suicide rate was recorded.


Russia has shown a pattern of increase and decrease in rank between years 1989 to 2007 and starting from the year 2007 Russia show a lower rank (out of top 3).

Correlation between different factors

The bubble chart below show the correlation between the suicides rate per 100,000 population , and the Gross Domestic Product(GDP) in thousands.

From the chart is show a weak positive relationship where richer countries are associated with higher rates of suicide.


Therefore, the suicide rate is not greatly impacted by a nation's level of development.

The bubble chart below show the correlation between Human Development Index (HDI) for year and the Gross Domestic Product(GDP) in thousands.

From the chart is show a strong positive relationship,countries with higher GDP per capita has higher HDI per year.

Besides, by slide over the year we can observe that both HDI for year and GDP per capita of Europe show an increasing pattern.
